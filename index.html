Section 03

Methodology

Three questions guide the index design: which behaviours best reveal civic quality, how to weight them, and how to make them comparable across countries.
Why these components

Most cross-national governance indices — the World Bank WGI, Transparency International CPI, World Justice Project Rule of Law — measure institutional quality as perceived by external experts. They capture what elites build. They say little about whether ordinary civic transactions are clean.

The CTI inverts the emphasis. The two highest-weighted components are Wallet Return Rate (Cohn et al., 2019) and Law & Order (2023) because they measure what actually happens at street level: whether strangers return lost property, whether women feel safe walking at night, whether bribes are demanded for routine services.

Behavioural outcomes — homicide, road deaths, waste management, terrorism — form the floor. A society where public order has collapsed cannot be high-trust regardless of its survey responses. Institutional indicators are included, but deliberately down-weighted to 6% of the composite.

Known limitations
  • East Asian survey bias. Japanese and Korean respondents systematically under-report interpersonal trust in WVS surveys due to cultural norms around modest self-presentation. Behavioural components partially correct for this, but attitudinal scores remain depressed relative to observed behaviour.
  • Cohn wallet study coverage. Only 40 countries were included in the 2019 experiment, with East Asia largely absent. Coverage skews toward developed economies — the most consequential gap in the dataset.
  • GCB vintage. The 2015/16/17 Global Corruption Barometer (119 countries) is the most recent with global scope. TI has since published only regional editions (Africa 2019, Asia 2020, EU 2021, etc.). These cannot be pooled without introducing systematic bias from differing instruments and fieldwork windows. Several high-income countries (Norway, Canada, USA, Switzerland) are absent from the 2017 edition and are unscored on this component.
  • LPI scope. The World Bank Logistics Performance Index covers transport infrastructure (ports, roads, rail, IT) as rated by freight forwarders — it excludes electricity and water supply, which the discontinued WEF GCI captured. Rankings are robust but the infrastructure component is now narrower in scope.
  • Shadow economy estimates. Schneider's MIMIC model is contested in the econometric literature. Country rankings are robust but the absolute percentages should be treated as approximations rather than measurements.
  • Property crime excluded. Police-reported theft data is confounded by reporting rates — high-trust societies report more theft, not less. The Law & Order index captures the experiential dimension more accurately through direct survey questions.
  • Micro-state scores. Liechtenstein, Monaco, San Marino and Andorra rank artificially high because they score on 5–6 components rather than 13, and those components happen to favour wealthy, stable micro-states. They are de-emphasised in the rankings.
Normalisation

All components are rescaled to a 0–100 range before weighting, so each contributes a comparable quantum regardless of its native units.

WGI scores (Corruption Control, Government Effectiveness) run natively from −2.5 to +2.5. These are linearly mapped to 0–100.

Rate data with long right tails — homicide, road deaths, terrorism — are log-normalised. Without this, the handful of extreme-violence countries compress everything else into an indistinguishable mid-range.

Shadow Economy is linearly inverted from % of GDP, with a 5% floor acknowledging that some informal activity exists in every economy.

Infrastructure uses the World Bank Logistics Performance Index 2023 (1–5 scale), income-adjusted using the same residual method: each country's LPI score is compared against the expected score for its income bracket. A rich country with poor logistics infrastructure scores below 50; a middle-income country that invests unusually well scores above 50. This replaces the discontinued WEF Global Competitiveness Index 2019.

Survey-based inputs (Gallup, WVS %, Legatum SC, EPI, GCB %) are used directly, as they already express comparable 0–100 or percentage scales.

Section 04

What we measure

Thirteen indicators drawn from independent datasets. Each captures a different dimension of civic life — from a wallet left on a counter to a bribe demanded at a government office.
Institutional & Infrastructure  ·  17%
Corruption Control
3%World Bank WGI, 2023

The World Bank's Worldwide Governance Indicators assess how well governments prevent public officials from using their positions for private gain — whether courts can check executive power, whether contracts are enforced without bribes, whether public procurement is clean. The score combines expert assessments, business surveys, and citizen surveys.

This is deliberately down-weighted to just 3% because it measures what institutions look like from the outside — not what ordinary people actually experience. Elite-level governance quality is a real thing, but it is already partially captured in the bribery-free rate, which measures what happens at the counter, not the boardroom.

Government Effectiveness
3%World Bank WGI, 2023

Also from the World Bank WGI, this measures the perceived quality of the civil service — whether the bureaucracy is competent, whether policies are well-designed and actually implemented, whether governments can deliver public services reliably. A high score means the trains run on time and licences are issued without delay.

Like corruption control, this is kept at 3% because expert-perceived institutional quality is a distant proxy for what citizens experience. A highly effective government can still preside over a low-trust society if that effectiveness is not felt at street level — and vice versa.

Infrastructure
11%World Bank LPI, 2023

The World Bank's Logistics Performance Index surveys international freight forwarders in 138 countries, asking them to rate the quality of ports, roads, railways, and IT infrastructure on a 1–5 scale. It is the broadest comparable infrastructure quality dataset available following the discontinuation of the WEF Global Competitiveness Index after 2019.

This component uses the income-adjusted residual — the gap between a country's actual LPI score and the score expected for its wealth level. A rich country with poor infrastructure scores below the midpoint; a middle-income country that has invested unusually well scores above it. This isolates civic investment in shared public goods from the simple fact of being wealthy.

Order & Fiscal  ·  18%
Shadow Economy
6%Schneider & Medina, IMF 2018

The shadow economy is economic activity that is informal, untaxed, and outside official systems — from cash-in-hand tradespeople to large-scale tax evasion. Economists estimate its size indirectly, using signals like electricity consumption and demand for cash relative to what official GDP would predict.

A large shadow economy indicates widespread distrust of formal institutions: people and businesses opt out of official systems because those systems are burdensome, corrupt, or simply do not function. Countries with small shadow economies — below 10% of GDP — tend to have high voluntary compliance with tax and regulation, which requires a baseline of trust in the state.

Law & Order
12%Gallup, 2023

Gallup's annual Law and Order Index surveys over 140 countries, asking people three questions: did you feel safe walking home alone at night? Was property stolen from you? Were you assaulted or mugged? The index combines confidence in local police with personal experience of safety.

This is not police-reported crime data, which is heavily distorted by reporting rates. It is what people actually experience. A country where crimes go unreported because no one trusts the police will show low crime statistics and a low Law & Order score simultaneously — the index captures the reality, not the paperwork.

Attitudinal Trust  ·  16%
Interpersonal Trust
8%World Values Survey Wave 7, 2017–22

The World Values Survey — the largest academic survey of human values ever conducted — asks one deceptively simple question: "Generally speaking, would you say that most people can be trusted, or that you need to be very careful in dealing with people?"

The share who answer "most people can be trusted" varies from below 5% in some countries to above 75% in others. This single number predicts an enormous range of social outcomes: economic growth, quality of institutions, political stability, even health. Societies where strangers trust each other can cooperate on problems that low-trust societies cannot.

Social Capital
8%Legatum Prosperity Index, 2023

Social capital is the informal glue that holds a society together: the density of networks, the willingness to volunteer and donate, the habit of helping strangers, the ability to rely on family and friends in a crisis. The Legatum Prosperity Index aggregates survey data on all of these into a single social capital score.

Where interpersonal trust measures an attitude — whether you believe strangers are trustworthy — social capital measures behaviour: whether people actually show up for each other. Both matter. A society can have high stated trust but weak civic participation, or dense community networks alongside high mistrust of outsiders.

Civic Honesty  ·  22%
Wallet Return Rate
12%Cohn et al., Science 2019

In 2019, behavioural economists left 17,303 wallets — each containing a small amount of cash, a few business cards, and a grocery list — at banks, hotels, police stations, theatres and post offices across 40 countries. They then recorded how often the wallet was returned with the money intact.

It is probably the purest field test of civic honesty ever conducted. No cameras, no accountability — just whether a stranger, finding someone else's wallet, does the honest thing when there is nothing to stop them keeping it.

Bribery-Free Rate
10%TI Global Corruption Barometer, 2017

The Transparency International Global Corruption Barometer surveys ordinary people — not executives or experts — about their direct experience of corruption. The question is simple: in the past year, did anyone ask you for a bribe? The bribery-free rate is the share of people who went through the year without being asked. The 2015/16/17 edition covered 119 countries and 162,000 respondents.

This is street-level corruption, not elite corruption. It captures whether the clerk at the vehicle registration office expects an envelope, whether the police officer stopping your car expects something in return for letting you go, whether a hospital requires unofficial payment before treating you.

Outcomes  ·  27%
Homicide Rate
7%UNODC, 2021–23

Intentional homicide per 100,000 people, from UN Office on Drugs and Crime data. Homicide is the most reliably comparable violent crime statistic across countries: bodies get counted, and the definition is consistent. All other crime data is fatally distorted by what gets reported.

A high homicide rate is a signal of fundamental civic breakdown — whether that is gang violence, state violence, domestic violence, or conflict. It sets a floor on how high a civic trust score can go.

Road Deaths
6%WHO Road Safety Report, 2021

Traffic fatalities per 100,000 people, from the WHO. Road death rates reflect a combination of infrastructure quality, the strictness and enforcement of traffic law, and the shared social norms around driving. Countries where overtaking on blind corners is routine, where seatbelts are ignored, and where speed limits are treated as suggestions score poorly.

High road mortality is not just a safety failure — it is evidence of a civic commons that is poorly maintained and poorly governed. Countries with very low road death rates (Norway, Sweden, Switzerland) have achieved this through decades of coordinated investment in both engineering and culture.

Waste Management
6%Yale Environmental Performance Index, 2022

Yale's Environmental Performance Index measures how effectively countries manage municipal solid waste — collection rates, controlled disposal, recycling infrastructure. Littered streets, illegal dumping, and open landfills are the visible face of a society that has not collectively maintained its public spaces.

Waste management is a particularly good civic indicator because it is almost entirely a matter of shared norms and collective governance, not geography or wealth. Countries at similar income levels can differ dramatically in how they treat common spaces — that difference is culture, not resources.

Terrorism Index
8%IEP Global Terrorism Index, 2023

The Institute for Economics and Peace tracks every recorded terrorist incident globally — frequency, lethality, and the groups responsible. The score reflects the overall impact of terrorism on a country in a given year.

Chronic political violence and civic trust are incompatible. Countries with near-zero scores — Iceland, Japan, most of Northern Europe — have essentially eliminated terrorism as a feature of daily life. Countries at the other end are places where violence has become normalised as a method of settling political disputes.

Section 05

About

The Civic Trust Index is an independent research project. All component datasets are publicly available from the sources listed on the opening page.
How to read this

Higher scores indicate cleaner streets, safer nights, honest transactions, and better public maintenance. Lower scores reflect the daily cost of low civic trust.

Use the sidebar filters to toggle components. The map and rankings update live — you can isolate Civic Honesty alone or check how sensitive a country's rank is to the wallet return data.

Hover any country for its full component breakdown — raw values, normalised scores, source and year. The one-sentence summary highlights the most distinctive feature of that country's profile.

Countries with fewer than 8 of 13 components are shown at reduced opacity in rankings. Their scores are valid but rest on a thinner data base.

Frequently asked
Why do Nordic countries rank so high?

Because they score consistently well across every category — not just institutions. Denmark, Norway and Finland top the Law & Order index, have near-zero bribery rates, low homicide, high wallet return rates, and excellent waste management. No single component carries them; they perform well on all simultaneously.

Why does the US rank lower than its WGI position?

The CTI weights outcomes heavily. The US has strong institutions but elevated homicide, road deaths, and terrorism scores that the WGI doesn't penalise. The CTI measures what people actually experience, not what experts perceive.

Why isn't CPI just used instead?

The TI Corruption Perceptions Index measures elite-level corruption perception. It doesn't capture whether a street-level bribe is needed to register a vehicle — which is what the GCB bribery rate measures and why both are included at different weights.

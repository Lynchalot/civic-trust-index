Standard rankings will tell you Japan and Mexico have both grown richer over the past decade. They won't tell you that in Japan, someone who finds a wallet stuffed with cash will almost certainly hand it in to the nearest police box. In Mexico, they almost certainly won't.

That gap — between what income figures show and what daily life actually feels like — is what this index tracks. Not whether governments are competent. Whether the street outside is clean. Whether you need to bribe someone to register a car. Whether strangers can be trusted.

Section 02

Methodology

Three questions guide the index design: which behaviours best reveal civic quality, how to weight them, and how to make them comparable across countries.
Why these components

Most cross-national governance indices — the World Bank WGI, Transparency International CPI, World Justice Project Rule of Law — measure institutional quality as perceived by external experts. They capture what elites build. They say little about whether ordinary civic transactions are clean.

The CTI inverts the emphasis. The two highest-weighted components are Wallet Return Rate (Cohn et al., 2019) and Law & Order (2023) because they measure what actually happens at street level: whether strangers return lost property, whether women feel safe walking at night, whether bribes are demanded for routine services.

Behavioural outcomes — homicide, road deaths, waste management, terrorism — form the floor. A society where public order has collapsed cannot be high-trust regardless of its survey responses. Institutional indicators are included, but deliberately down-weighted to 6% of the composite.

Known limitations
  • East Asian survey bias. Japanese and Korean respondents systematically under-report interpersonal trust in WVS surveys due to cultural norms around modest self-presentation. Behavioural components partially correct for this, but attitudinal scores remain depressed relative to observed behaviour.
  • Cohn wallet study coverage. Only 40 countries were included in the 2019 experiment, with East Asia largely absent. Coverage skews toward developed economies — the most consequential gap in the dataset.
  • GCB vintage. The 2013 Global Corruption Barometer wave is the most recent with comprehensive cross-country coverage. Regional surveys from 2019–20 exist but cannot be pooled without introducing systematic bias.
  • Shadow economy estimates. Schneider's MIMIC model is contested in the econometric literature. Country rankings are robust but the absolute percentages should be treated as approximations rather than measurements.
  • Property crime excluded. Police-reported theft data is confounded by reporting rates — high-trust societies report more theft, not less. The Law & Order index captures the experiential dimension more accurately through direct survey questions.
  • Micro-state scores. Liechtenstein, Monaco, San Marino and Andorra rank artificially high because they score on 5–6 components rather than 13, and those components happen to favour wealthy, stable micro-states. They are de-emphasised in the rankings.
Normalisation

All components are rescaled to a 0–100 range before weighting, so each contributes a comparable quantum regardless of its native units.

WGI scores (Corruption Control, Government Effectiveness) run natively from −2.5 to +2.5. These are linearly mapped to 0–100.

Rate data with long right tails — homicide, road deaths, terrorism — are log-normalised. Without this, the handful of extreme-violence countries compress everything else into an indistinguishable mid-range.

Shadow Economy is linearly inverted from % of GDP, with a 5% floor acknowledging that some informal activity exists in every economy.

Infrastructure is the residual after subtracting the expected score for each country's income bracket. This isolates civic investment in public goods from the confounding effect of national wealth — a rich country with poor roads scores lower than a middle-income country with unusually good ones.

Survey-based inputs (Gallup, WVS %, Legatum SC, EPI, GCB %) are used directly, as they already express comparable 0–100 or percentage scales.

Section 03

About

The Civic Trust Index is an independent research project. All component datasets are publicly available from the sources listed on the opening page.
How to read this

Higher scores indicate cleaner streets, safer nights, honest transactions, and better public maintenance. Lower scores reflect the daily cost of low civic trust.

Use the sidebar filters to toggle components. The map and rankings update live — you can isolate Civic Honesty alone or check how sensitive a country's rank is to the wallet return data.

Hover any country for its full component breakdown — raw values, normalised scores, source and year. The one-sentence summary highlights the most distinctive feature of that country's profile.

Countries with fewer than 8 of 13 components are shown at reduced opacity in rankings. Their scores are valid but rest on a thinner data base.

Frequently asked
Why do Nordic countries rank so high?

Because they score consistently well across every category — not just institutions. Denmark, Norway and Finland top the Law & Order index, have near-zero bribery rates, low homicide, high wallet return rates, and excellent waste management. No single component carries them; they perform well on all simultaneously.

Why does the US rank lower than its WGI position?

The CTI weights outcomes heavily. The US has strong institutions but elevated homicide, road deaths, and terrorism scores that the WGI doesn't penalise. The CTI measures what people actually experience, not what experts perceive.

Why isn't CPI just used instead?

The TI Corruption Perceptions Index measures elite-level corruption perception. It doesn't capture whether a street-level bribe is needed to register a vehicle — which is what the GCB bribery rate measures and why both are included at different weights.
